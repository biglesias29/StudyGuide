Introduction & Research Methods

What is psychology? What is critical thinking?▶

Psychology is the scientific study of behavior and mental processes.

Critical thinking means evaluating claims using evidence, logic, and skepticism rather than assumptions. It involves: asking questions, examining evidence, analyzing assumptions, considering alternatives, and drawing careful conclusions.

Contemporary schools of thought in psychology▶
SchoolCore Idea
Psychoanalytic/PsychodynamicUnconscious conflicts (Freud); early childhood shapes personality
BehaviorismOnly observable behavior matters; shaped by environment/conditioning (Watson, Skinner)
Gestalt"The whole is greater than the sum of its parts" — perception is organized holistically
HumanisticFocus on free will, growth, self-actualization (Maslow, Rogers)
CognitiveMental processes (thinking, memory, problem-solving) shape behavior
Elements of a true experiment: IV, DV, groups, random assignment▶
  • Independent Variable (IV): What the researcher manipulates (the cause)
  • Dependent Variable (DV): What is measured (the effect/outcome)
  • Experimental group: Receives the treatment/manipulation
  • Control group: Does NOT receive the treatment (baseline for comparison)
  • Random assignment: Participants randomly placed into groups — this controls for pre-existing differences and allows cause-and-effect conclusions
⚠️ Key: Only true experiments (with random assignment) can establish cause and effect.
Hypothesis vs. Operational Definition▶

Hypothesis: A testable prediction about the relationship between variables. Example: "Students who sleep 8 hours will score higher on exams."

Operational definition: How a variable is specifically measured or defined in the study. Example: "Sleep" = hours logged via wristband tracker; "score" = percent correct on a 50-item test.

Correlations: directionality & third variable problems▶

A correlation shows a relationship between two variables — it does NOT prove causation.

  • Directionality problem: We don't know which variable causes the other. (Does depression cause inactivity, or does inactivity cause depression?)
  • Third variable problem: A hidden third variable may cause both. (Ice cream sales and drowning rates correlate — both caused by hot weather.)

Experiment vs. Correlation: An experiment manipulates a variable and uses random assignment → can establish cause. A correlational study just measures variables as they exist → cannot establish cause.

Surveys, observational studies, case studies; sample vs. population▶
  • Survey: Self-report questionnaire given to many people
  • Observational study: Researcher watches and records behavior without intervening
  • Case study: In-depth study of one individual or group

Population: The entire group of interest. Sample: The subset of the population actually studied. Good samples are representative of the population.

Neuroscience & Biological Foundations

Parts of a neuron and their functions▶
PartFunction
DendritesReceive incoming signals from other neurons
Cell body (soma)Processes signals; contains nucleus
AxonCarries electrical signal away from cell body
Myelin sheathFatty coating that insulates axon; speeds signal transmission
Terminal budsRelease neurotransmitters into synapse
SynapseThe gap between neurons where communication occurs

Action potential: The electrical charge that travels down the axon when a neuron fires. It's all-or-nothing.

Neurotransmitters: Chemical messengers released from terminal buds that cross the synapse and bind to the next neuron's receptors.

The nervous system — all parts and functions▶
Central Nervous System (CNS): Brain + Spinal cord
Peripheral Nervous System (PNS): All nerves outside brain/spinal cord

The PNS splits into:

  • Somatic NS: Controls voluntary muscle movements; carries sensory info to CNS
  • Autonomic NS: Controls involuntary functions (heart rate, digestion, glands)

The Autonomic NS splits into:

  • Sympathetic: "Fight or flight" — accelerates heart rate, dilates pupils, slows digestion
  • Parasympathetic: "Rest and digest" — slows heart, constricts pupils, promotes digestion
Brain lobes and cortex regions▶
StructureFunction
Frontal lobe / Motor cortexPlanning, decision-making, personality; voluntary movement
Parietal lobe / Somatosensory cortexProcesses touch, pain, temperature, body position
Occipital lobe / Primary visual cortexProcesses visual information
Temporal lobe / Primary auditory cortexLanguage, hearing, memory (contains hippocampus)
Corpus callosumBundle of fibers connecting left and right hemispheres
Thalamus, hypothalamus, amygdala, hippocampus▶
  • Thalamus: Relay station — routes sensory info to correct brain regions
  • Hypothalamus: Regulates drives (hunger, thirst, sex, temperature); controls pituitary gland
  • Amygdala: Emotional processing, especially fear and aggression
  • Hippocampus: Forming new long-term memories; spatial navigation
Pons, medulla, substantia nigra, cerebellum & neuroplasticity▶
  • Medulla: Controls vital functions — breathing, heart rate, blood pressure
  • Pons: Involved in sleep, arousal, relaying signals; connects cerebrum to cerebellum
  • Substantia nigra: Produces dopamine; involved in movement (damaged in Parkinson's)
  • Cerebellum: Coordination, balance, fine motor movements

Neuroplasticity: The brain's ability to reorganize and form new neural connections throughout life — especially in response to learning or injury.

Endocrine system: glands and hormones▶

The endocrine system communicates via hormones (chemical messengers) secreted by glands directly into the bloodstream — slower but longer-lasting than neural signals.

Key glands: pituitary (master gland), adrenal (stress hormones like cortisol/adrenaline), thyroid, pancreas, gonads (sex hormones).

03

Sensation & Perception

Sensation vs. Perception; Transduction▶

Sensation: Detection of physical stimuli by sensory organs (raw data input).

Perception: The brain's interpretation and organization of those signals (making meaning).

Transduction: Converting physical stimulus energy (light, sound waves, etc.) into electrical signals the brain can process.

Bottom-up vs. Top-down processing▶
  • Bottom-up processing: Starts with raw sensory data, builds up to perception. Data-driven. Example: reading letter by letter.
  • Top-down processing: Uses prior knowledge, context, and expectations to interpret sensory input. Example: reading a misspelled word correctly because you know what it "should" say.
Absolute threshold, difference threshold (JND), sensory adaptation▶
  • Absolute threshold: The minimum stimulus intensity detectable 50% of the time
  • Difference threshold (JND – Just Noticeable Difference): The minimum change in stimulus needed to notice a difference 50% of the time
  • Sensory adaptation: Diminished sensitivity to a constant stimulus over time (e.g., you stop noticing background noise)
Kinesthetic sense, vestibular sense; monocular vs. binocular depth cues▶
  • Kinesthetic sense: Awareness of body position and movement of limbs
  • Vestibular sense: Sense of balance and spatial orientation (inner ear)

Depth cues:

  • Monocular cues: Work with one eye — linear perspective, relative size, overlap, texture gradient, motion parallax
  • Binocular cues: Require both eyes — retinal disparity (slightly different images from each eye) and convergence (eyes turning inward for close objects)
04

Learning

Classical conditioning — all key terms with examples▶
Classical conditioning = learning through association. An originally neutral stimulus comes to produce a response after being paired with a stimulus that naturally causes that response.
TermDefinitionPavlov Example
Unconditioned Stimulus (US)Naturally triggers responseFood
Unconditioned Response (UR)Natural, unlearned response to USSalivation
Neutral Stimulus (NS)Initially produces no responseBell (before conditioning)
Conditioned Stimulus (CS)Previously neutral; now triggers response after learningBell (after conditioning)
Conditioned Response (CR)Learned response to CSSalivation to bell
  • Stimulus generalization: Responding to stimuli similar to the CS (dog conditioned to bell also responds to a buzzer)
  • Stimulus discrimination: Responding only to the specific CS, not similar stimuli
  • Extinction: CR disappears when CS is repeatedly presented without US
  • Spontaneous recovery: Extinguished CR reappears after a rest period
Operant conditioning — reinforcement, punishment, shaping▶
Operant conditioning = learning from consequences of behavior (Skinner). Behavior increases when reinforced, decreases when punished.
TypeWhat happensEffect on behaviorExample
Positive reinforcementAdd something pleasantIncreaseGive candy for good grades
Negative reinforcementRemove something unpleasantIncreaseFasten seatbelt to stop beeping
Positive punishmentAdd something unpleasantDecreaseExtra homework for misbehaving
Negative punishmentRemove something pleasantDecreaseTake away phone for bad behavior

Primary reinforcers: Innately satisfying (food, water, warmth). Secondary reinforcers: Learned value (money, grades, praise).

Shaping: Reinforcing successive approximations toward a target behavior. Example: rewarding a rat for moving toward a lever, then touching it, then pressing it.

Observational learning: Learning by watching others (Bandura's Bobo doll study). No direct reinforcement required.

Memory

Three types of memory — capacity and duration▶
TypeDurationCapacity
Sensory memoryFraction of a second (~0.5–3 sec)Large (all sensory input briefly)
Short-term memory (STM)~20–30 seconds without rehearsal7 ± 2 items (Miller's Law)
Long-term memory (LTM)Potentially unlimited / lifetimePotentially unlimited

Chunking: Grouping items into meaningful units to expand STM capacity. Example: remembering "FBI-CIA-NASA" as 3 chunks instead of 9 letters.

Types of long-term memory; rehearsal types▶
  • Semantic memory: General facts and knowledge ("Paris is the capital of France")
  • Episodic memory: Personal experiences and events ("My first day of college")
  • Procedural memory: Skills and how to do things ("How to ride a bike")
  • Maintenance rehearsal: Simple repetition to keep info in STM (reciting a phone number). Less effective for LTM.
  • Elaborative rehearsal: Connecting new info to existing knowledge. Far more effective for long-term retention.

Context-dependent memory: Recall is better when the retrieval environment matches the encoding environment (studying and testing in the same room).

Amnesia types; reasons we forget▶
  • Anterograde amnesia: Cannot form NEW memories after brain injury (can still recall old memories)
  • Retrograde amnesia: Cannot recall memories BEFORE brain injury

Why we forget:

  • Blocking: Memory exists but can't be retrieved (tip-of-the-tongue phenomenon)
  • Misattribution: Remembering something correctly but attributing it to the wrong source
  • Suggestibility: Memories altered by misleading info from others (misinformation effect)

Lifespan Development

Three domains of development; teratogens; attachment styles▶

Three domains: Physical (body, brain, motor skills) | Cognitive (thinking, memory, language) | Social-Emotional (relationships, emotions, personality)

Teratogens: Environmental agents that harm prenatal development (alcohol, drugs, radiation, certain infections). Damage depends on timing and dosage.

Attachment styles (Ainsworth's Strange Situation):
• Secure: Distressed when caregiver leaves, happy reunion, uses caregiver as safe base
• Ambivalent (anxious): Very distressed at separation, ambivalent/angry at reunion — not easily soothed
• Avoidant: Little distress at separation, ignores/avoids caregiver at reunion

07

Personality

Freud's psychodynamic theory — 3 structures & defense mechanisms▶
  • Id: Unconscious, primitive drives (pleasure principle) — wants immediate gratification
  • Ego: Mediator; operates on reality principle — finds realistic ways to satisfy the id
  • Superego: Internalized moral standards; guilt when rules are broken

Defense mechanisms: Unconscious strategies the ego uses to reduce anxiety (repression, denial, projection, rationalization).

Big Five (OCEAN); locus of control; self-actualization; key terms▶
Big Five (OCEAN):
Openness | Conscientiousness | Extraversion | Agreeableness | Neuroticism
  • Self-actualization: Realizing one's full potential (Maslow/Rogers — top of hierarchy of needs)
  • Internal locus of control: Belief that you control your own outcomes
  • External locus of control: Belief that outside forces (luck, others) control outcomes
  • Unconditional positive regard: Accepting someone fully without conditions (Rogers)
  • Self-schema: Mental framework/beliefs about oneself
  • Reciprocal determinism (Bandura): Behavior, personal factors, and environment all mutually influence each other

Tests: Self-report tests (e.g., MMPI) — questionnaires. Projective tests (e.g., Rorschach inkblot, TAT) — ambiguous stimuli reveal unconscious thoughts.

08

Social Psychology

Attribution errors; conformity, compliance, obedience▶
  • Personal (internal) attribution: Behavior caused by the person's character/traits
  • Situational (external) attribution: Behavior caused by the situation
  • Fundamental attribution error: We OVER-attribute others' behavior to personal factors and UNDER-attribute to situational factors
  • Conformity: Adjusting behavior to match group norms (Asch line studies)
  • Compliance: Agreeing to a direct request from someone (not necessarily authority)
  • Obedience: Following orders from an authority figure
Milgram's obedience study: Participants administered (fake) electric shocks to strangers when ordered by an authority figure in a lab coat. ~65% went to maximum voltage — showed power of authority over behavior.
Cognitive dissonance; bystander effect; stereotypes, prejudice, discrimination▶
  • Cognitive dissonance: Discomfort from holding conflicting beliefs or behaving inconsistently with beliefs → motivates attitude change
  • Bystander intervention effect: People are less likely to help in an emergency when others are present (diffusion of responsibility)
  • Stereotype: Generalized belief about a group
  • Prejudice: Negative attitude/feeling toward a group
  • Discrimination: Negative behavior/action toward a group
  • Self-fulfilling prophecy: A belief that causes itself to become true through behavior (teacher expects student to fail → student fails)
09

Psychological Disorders

DSM-5; diathesis-stress model; biopsychosocial approach▶

DSM-5: Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, 5th ed. — the standard classification system for psychological disorders used by clinicians.

Diathesis-stress model: Disorders arise from a combination of biological predisposition (diathesis) + environmental stressors. Neither alone is sufficient.

Biopsychosocial approach: Considers biological (genetics, brain), psychological (thoughts, emotions), and social (culture, relationships) factors together.

Anxiety disorders: GAD, panic disorder, specific phobias▶
  • Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD): Persistent, excessive worry about multiple life areas for 6+ months; restlessness, fatigue, difficulty concentrating
  • Panic Disorder: Recurrent unexpected panic attacks (intense fear, chest pain, shortness of breath, feeling of doom) + worry about future attacks
  • Specific Phobia: Intense, irrational fear of a specific object/situation that is disproportionate to actual danger
OCD; Major Depressive Disorder; learned helplessness▶
  • OCD — Obsessions: Unwanted, intrusive, recurring thoughts (e.g., fear of contamination)
  • OCD — Compulsions: Repetitive behaviors performed to reduce obsession anxiety (e.g., excessive hand-washing)

Major Depressive Episode symptoms: Depressed mood, loss of interest/pleasure, weight changes, sleep disturbance, fatigue, feelings of worthlessness, difficulty concentrating, thoughts of death — lasting 2+ weeks.

Learned helplessness: After repeated uncontrollable negative events, a person gives up trying to change their situation — linked to depression.

Bipolar disorder; schizophrenia; personality disorders; neurodevelopmental disorders▶

Bipolar disorder: Episodes of mania (elevated mood, grandiosity, decreased need for sleep, impulsivity) alternating with depression.

Schizophrenia:

  • Positive symptoms: ADDED experiences — hallucinations (false perceptions, e.g., hearing voices) and delusions (false beliefs, e.g., being persecuted)
  • Negative symptoms: REDUCED functions — flat affect, poverty of speech, social withdrawal, lack of motivation

Borderline PD: Unstable relationships, identity, and moods; fear of abandonment; impulsivity; self-harm

Antisocial PD: Persistent disregard for others' rights; deceitfulness, lack of remorse, impulsivity, aggression

ASD (Autism Spectrum): Deficits in social communication; restricted, repetitive behaviors; varies in severity

ADHD: Inattention and/or hyperactivity-impulsivity that impairs functioning; onset in childhood

Therapy

Biological therapy vs. psychotherapy; major schools▶

Biological therapy: Treats disorders with physical/medical means — medications (antidepressants, antipsychotics), ECT, TMS.

Psychotherapy: Psychological treatment involving talking and behavioral techniques.

TypeFocus
Psychoanalysis/PsychodynamicExplore unconscious conflicts, early childhood; free association, dream analysis
HumanisticFoster growth, self-acceptance; unconditional positive regard (Rogers)
Behavior therapyChange maladaptive behaviors using conditioning principles
CBTChange negative thoughts AND behaviors; most evidence-based
Rogers' client-centered therapy; behavior therapies; CBT; group therapy▶

Rogers' client-centered therapy: Therapist provides unconditional positive regard, empathy, and genuineness. Goal: help client reach self-actualization and self-acceptance.

Behavior therapy techniques:

  • Systematic desensitization: Gradually expose client to feared stimulus while maintaining relaxation (treats phobias)
  • Exposure and response prevention: Confront feared stimulus and resist compulsive response (used for OCD)
  • Operant procedures: Use reinforcement/punishment to shape target behaviors (token economies)

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT): Identifies and challenges distorted thinking patterns; changes both cognitions and behaviors. Most empirically supported therapy.

Group therapy: Multiple clients treated together. Benefits: social support, learn from others, feel less alone, cost-effective, practice social skills.
