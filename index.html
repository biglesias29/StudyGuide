01

Intro & Research Methods

What is psychology? What is critical thinking?▶

Psychology: Scientific study of behavior and mental processes.

Critical thinking: Evaluating claims using evidence, logic, and skepticism — asking questions, examining evidence, analyzing assumptions, considering alternatives, and drawing careful conclusions.

Contemporary schools of thought▶
SchoolCore Idea
PsychodynamicUnconscious conflicts and early childhood shape personality (Freud)
BehaviorismOnly observable behavior matters; shaped by conditioning (Watson, Skinner)
Gestalt"Whole > sum of parts" — perception is organized holistically
HumanisticFree will, growth, self-actualization (Maslow, Rogers)
CognitiveMental processes (thinking, memory) shape behavior
IV, DV, groups, random assignment▶
  • IV: What the researcher manipulates (the cause)
  • DV: What is measured (the effect)
  • Experimental group: Receives the treatment
  • Control group: No treatment — baseline comparison
  • Random assignment: Controls pre-existing differences → allows cause-and-effect conclusions
⚠️ Only true experiments (with random assignment) can establish cause and effect.
Hypothesis vs. Operational Definition▶

Hypothesis: A testable prediction about the relationship between variables. "Students who sleep 8 hrs score higher on exams."

Operational definition: How a variable is specifically measured. "Sleep" = wristband hours; "score" = % correct on 50-item test.

Placebo effect, double-blind design, replication▶

Placebo effect: A participant's belief that they are receiving treatment causes real psychological or physical changes — even without the actual treatment.

Double-blind design: Neither the participants NOR the researchers know who is in the experimental vs. control group — eliminates both participant and researcher bias.

Replication: Repeating a study to verify its results. A finding is only considered reliable when it has been replicated by independent researchers. Peer review ensures research quality before publication.

Correlations: directionality & third variable▶

A correlation shows a relationship — NOT causation.

  • Directionality: Can't tell which variable causes the other
  • Third variable: A hidden cause drives both (hot weather → ice cream sales AND drowning rates)
Survey, observational study, case study; sample vs. population▶
  • Survey: Self-report questionnaire to many people
  • Observational study: Watch behavior without intervening
  • Case study: In-depth study of one individual

Population: Entire group of interest. Sample: Subset actually studied. Good samples are representative.

03

Sensation & Perception

Sensation vs. Perception; Transduction▶

Sensation: Detection of physical stimuli by sensory organs — the raw data before any interpretation.

Perception: The brain's active process of interpreting and organizing sensory signals into meaningful experiences.

Transduction: Converting physical stimulus energy (light, sound waves, pressure) into electrical signals the brain can process.

Bottom-up vs. Top-down processing▶
  • Bottom-up: Starts with raw sensory data → builds to perception. Data-driven, no prior assumptions. Example: sounding out an unfamiliar word letter by letter.
  • Top-down: Uses prior knowledge & expectations to interpret input. Example: reading a misspelled word correctly because context fills in the gap.
Absolute threshold, JND, sensory adaptation▶
  • Absolute threshold: Min stimulus detectable 50% of the time
  • JND (Just Noticeable Difference / difference threshold): Min change in stimulus noticed 50% of the time
  • Sensory adaptation: Diminished sensitivity to a constant stimulus over time (e.g., you stop noticing background music)
Gestalt principles of perception▶

Gestalt principles describe how we organize sensory input into meaningful wholes — the brain imposes structure on what it perceives.

  • Figure-ground: We perceive one element as the "figure" (foreground) and the rest as "ground" (background)
  • Proximity: Objects close together are perceived as a group
  • Similarity: Objects that look alike are grouped together
  • Closure: We mentally fill in gaps to perceive complete shapes
  • Continuity: We perceive smooth, continuous patterns rather than abrupt changes
Kinesthetic & vestibular sense; depth cues▶
  • Kinesthetic sense: Body position and limb movement awareness
  • Vestibular sense: Balance and spatial orientation (inner ear)
  • Monocular cues: One eye — linear perspective, relative size, overlap, texture gradient, motion parallax
  • Binocular cues: Both eyes — retinal disparity (each eye gets slightly different image) + convergence (eyes turn inward for close objects)
04

Learning

Classical conditioning — all key terms▶
Learning through association: a neutral stimulus comes to produce a response after being paired with a stimulus that naturally causes that response.
TermPavlov Example
US — natural triggerFood
UR — natural responseSalivation to food
NS — no response yetBell (before training)
CS — learned triggerBell (after training)
CR — learned responseSalivation to bell
  • Generalization: Respond to stimuli similar to CS
  • Discrimination: Respond only to specific CS
  • Extinction: CR disappears when CS presented without US
  • Spontaneous recovery: Extinguished CR reappears after rest
Operant conditioning — reinforcement & punishment▶
TypeActionEffectExample
+ ReinforcementAdd pleasant↑ behaviorCandy for good grades
− ReinforcementRemove unpleasant↑ behaviorSeatbelt stops beeping
+ PunishmentAdd unpleasant↓ behaviorExtra homework for misbehaving
− PunishmentRemove pleasant↓ behaviorTake away phone

Primary reinforcers: Innately satisfying (food, water). Secondary reinforcers: Learned value (money, praise).

Continuous reinforcement: Reinforced every time → fast learning, fast extinction. Partial reinforcement: Reinforced sometimes → slower learning, very resistant to extinction.

Shaping: Reinforcing successive approximations toward a target behavior.

Token economy: A behavior modification system where desired behaviors earn tokens that can be exchanged for privileges or rewards — widely used in schools, therapy, and institutions.

Observational learning; latent learning▶

Observational learning (Bandura): Learning by watching and imitating others — no direct reinforcement required. Demonstrated by Bandura's Bobo doll study, in which children who watched an adult hit an inflatable doll reproduced the aggressive behavior.

Latent learning (Tolman): Learning that occurs without reinforcement and is not immediately demonstrated — it remains hidden until there is a reason to use it. Example: rats who explored a maze without reward still learned its layout, performing better than unrewarded controls once food was added.

Why it matters: Latent learning shows that reinforcement is not necessary for learning to occur — challenging strict behaviorism.
05

Memory

Three stages of memory: encoding, storage, retrieval▶
  • Encoding: The process of getting information into memory — converting sensory input into a form the brain can store. Deeper, more meaningful encoding = stronger memory.
  • Storage: Retaining encoded information over time in sensory, short-term, or long-term memory.
  • Retrieval: Accessing and bringing stored information back to conscious awareness. Can be aided by cues that match the original encoding context.
Forgetting can occur at any stage — poor encoding, decay during storage, or failure to retrieve.
The four memory stores — what they hold, how long, and how much▶
StoreDurationCapacityWhat it holds
Sensory Memory0.5–3 secVery large — all incoming sensory dataRaw, unprocessed sensory input (sights, sounds, touch) — most fades instantly unless attended to
Short-Term Memory (STM)~20–30 sec without rehearsal7 ± 2 items (Miller's Law)Information currently being consciously thought about — actively held and manipulated
Working MemoryAs long as actively maintainedVery limited (4 chunks, per Cowan)A subset of STM — the "mental workspace" where we actively process, manipulate, and integrate information (e.g., doing math in your head, holding a sentence while reading)
Long-Term Memory (LTM)Potentially a lifetimeVirtually unlimitedAll stored knowledge, skills, experiences, and memories not currently in conscious awareness
Flow of information: Sensory input → Sensory Memory → (if attended to) → STM / Working Memory → (if encoded deeply) → Long-Term Memory → (retrieved when needed) → back into Working Memory

Chunking: Grouping items into meaningful units to expand STM/working memory. FBI-CIA-NASA = 3 chunks, not 9 letters.

Sensory memory in depth — iconic & echoic memory▶

Sensory memory holds an exact, unprocessed copy of sensory experience for a very brief moment — acting as a buffer before the brain decides what to pay attention to.

  • Iconic memory (visual): Photographic-like snapshot of a visual scene lasting ~0.5 seconds. Studied by Sperling — participants could briefly "see" an entire grid of letters but could only report a few before the image faded.
  • Echoic memory (auditory): A brief echo of sound lasting ~3–4 seconds — longer than iconic. This is why you can "replay" the last few words someone just said even if you weren't paying attention.

Most sensory information is lost here unless selective attention flags it for transfer to short-term/working memory.

Working memory vs. STM — what's the difference?▶

Short-term memory (STM) is the older, simpler concept — a temporary holding space for a small amount of information (~7 items for ~30 seconds).

Working memory (Baddeley's model) is the updated, more detailed model. It is not just a passive storage space — it actively processes and manipulates information. It has four components:

  • Central executive: The "boss" — directs attention, coordinates the other components, and makes decisions
  • Phonological loop: Holds and rehearses verbal and auditory information (inner voice + inner ear). Used when reading, listening, or remembering a phone number.
  • Visuospatial sketchpad: Holds and manipulates visual and spatial information — used for mental imagery, navigation, and imagining how furniture looks rearranged.
  • Episodic buffer: Temporarily links working memory information with LTM, integrating information across all systems into a coherent "episode."
Real-world uses of working memory: Following multi-step directions, doing mental arithmetic, understanding a complex sentence, holding a chess position in mind, or cooking multiple dishes at once.
Long-term memory — explicit vs. implicit; all subtypes▶

LTM is divided into explicit (declarative) — memories you can consciously recall and state — and implicit (nondeclarative) — memories expressed through behavior without conscious awareness.

TypeSubtypeWhat it storesExample
ExplicitSemanticGeneral facts & world knowledge"Water boils at 100°C"
ExplicitEpisodicPersonal experiences & autobiographical eventsYour first day of school
ImplicitProceduralMotor skills & how-to knowledgeRiding a bike, typing
ImplicitPrimingExposure to one stimulus influences response to a later oneSeeing "doctor" makes you read "nurse" faster
ImplicitConditioned responsesClassically conditioned emotional/physical reactionsFear response to a dog after being bitten
⚠️ Key exam distinction: Amnesiac patients (like H.M.) lose explicit memory but retain implicit memory — they can learn new skills (procedural) without consciously remembering learning them.
LTM types; rehearsal types; context-dependent memory▶
  • Semantic: General facts ("Paris is in France")
  • Episodic: Personal experiences and events
  • Procedural: How-to skills (riding a bike) — often unconscious
  • Maintenance rehearsal: Simple repetition — keeps info in STM, weak for LTM
  • Elaborative rehearsal: Connecting new info to existing knowledge — strongest for LTM

Context-dependent memory: Recall is better when the retrieval environment matches the encoding environment (studying and testing in the same room).

Amnesia types; blocking, misattribution, suggestibility▶
  • Anterograde amnesia: Cannot form NEW memories after brain injury — old memories intact
  • Retrograde amnesia: Cannot recall memories from BEFORE a brain injury
  • Blocking (tip-of-the-tongue): Memory exists but temporarily cannot be retrieved
  • Misattribution: Correct memory, wrong source — remembering a fact but attributing it to the wrong person/event
  • Suggestibility (misinformation effect): Memories can be distorted by misleading post-event information — e.g., a leading question from a lawyer can alter eyewitness memory

Development

Three domains; teratogens; nature vs. nurture▶

Domains: Physical (body, brain, motor) | Cognitive (thinking, memory, language) | Social-Emotional (relationships, identity, emotions)

Teratogens: Environmental agents that harm prenatal development (alcohol, drugs, radiation). Damage depends on timing and dosage — critical periods are most vulnerable.

Nature vs. Nurture: The longstanding debate about whether behavior is primarily determined by genes (nature) or environment/experience (nurture). Modern view: both interact — genes set a range, environment shapes expression (gene-environment interaction).

Cross-sectional study: Compares different age groups at the same point in time — fast but can't track individual change. Longitudinal study: Follows the same group over time — tracks real development but slow and expensive.

Attachment styles (Ainsworth)▶
Secure: Distressed at separation, joyful at reunion, uses caregiver as safe base to explore
Ambivalent/Anxious: Very distressed at separation, angry/clingy at reunion — not soothed easily
Avoidant: Little distress at separation, ignores or avoids caregiver at reunion

Attachment style is shaped by caregiver responsiveness and has lifelong effects on relationships and emotional regulation.

Personality

Freud: Id, Ego, Superego & defense mechanisms▶
  • Id: Unconscious primitive drives — pleasure principle (wants immediate gratification)
  • Ego: Mediator — reality principle (finds realistic ways to satisfy id)
  • Superego: Internalized moral standards; produces guilt when violated

Defense mechanisms: Unconscious strategies the ego uses to reduce anxiety — repression (blocking out threatening thoughts), denial, projection (attributing own feelings to others), rationalization (making excuses).

Big Five (OCEAN); locus of control; key humanistic terms▶
OCEAN: Openness | Conscientiousness | Extraversion | Agreeableness | Neuroticism
  • Self-actualization: Reaching one's full potential (Maslow/Rogers)
  • Internal locus of control: You control your own outcomes
  • External locus of control: Outside forces (luck, others) control outcomes
  • Unconditional positive regard: Full acceptance without conditions (Rogers)
  • Reciprocal determinism (Bandura): Behavior, cognition, and environment mutually influence each other
Self-report tests vs. projective tests▶

Self-report tests (objective tests): Questionnaires where individuals rate or describe their own personality. Examples: MMPI (Minnesota Multiphasic Personality Inventory), Big Five questionnaires. Pros: standardized, reliable. Cons: subject to social desirability bias (people answer how they want to look).

Projective tests: Present ambiguous stimuli — the person "projects" their unconscious thoughts and feelings onto them. Examples: Rorschach inkblot test, TAT (Thematic Apperception Test). Pros: may reveal unconscious material. Cons: low reliability and validity; highly subjective to interpret.

08

Social Psychology

Attribution; conformity, compliance, obedience▶
  • Internal attribution: Behavior caused by the person's character/traits
  • External attribution: Behavior caused by the situation
  • Fundamental attribution error: Over-attribute others' behavior to personal factors; under-attribute to situational factors
  • Conformity: Matching group behavior (Asch line study)
  • Compliance: Following a direct request
  • Obedience: Following authority orders
Milgram: ~65% of participants delivered maximum (fake) electric shocks when ordered by an authority figure in a lab coat — demonstrated the power of situational authority over individual moral judgment.
Cognitive dissonance; bystander effect; prejudice & stereotypes▶
  • Cognitive dissonance: Discomfort from holding conflicting beliefs or acting against beliefs → motivates attitude change to restore consistency
  • Bystander effect: Less likely to help in an emergency when others are present — diffusion of responsibility. The more bystanders, the less any single person feels responsible.
  • Stereotype: Generalized belief about a group
  • Prejudice: Negative attitude/feeling toward a group
  • Discrimination: Negative behavior toward a group
  • Self-fulfilling prophecy: A belief that causes itself to become true through behavior it generates
09

Psychological Disorders

DSM-5; diathesis-stress model; biopsychosocial approach▶

DSM-5: Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders — standard classification system for psychological disorders used by clinicians.

Diathesis-stress model: Disorders arise from a biological predisposition (diathesis) + environmental stressors. Neither alone is sufficient — both are required.

Biopsychosocial approach: Considers biological (genetics, brain chemistry), psychological (thoughts, emotions, behavior), and social (culture, relationships, stressors) factors together.

Anxiety disorders: GAD, panic disorder, specific phobias▶
  • GAD: Persistent, excessive worry about multiple areas for 6+ months; fatigue, restlessness, difficulty concentrating
  • Panic Disorder: Recurrent unexpected panic attacks (intense fear, chest pain, shortness of breath, sense of doom) + ongoing worry about future attacks
  • Specific Phobia: Intense, irrational, persistent fear of a specific object or situation disproportionate to actual danger
OCD; Major Depressive Disorder; learned helplessness▶
  • OCD Obsessions: Unwanted, intrusive recurring thoughts (e.g., contamination fear)
  • OCD Compulsions: Repetitive behaviors to reduce obsession-related anxiety (e.g., excessive hand-washing)

MDD symptoms (5+ for 2+ weeks): Depressed mood, loss of interest/pleasure, weight/sleep changes, fatigue, worthlessness, poor concentration, thoughts of death.

Learned helplessness (Seligman): After repeated uncontrollable negative events, a person gives up trying to change their situation — a key cognitive model of depression.

Bipolar; schizophrenia; personality disorders; ADHD/ASD▶

Bipolar disorder: Alternating episodes of mania (elevated mood, grandiosity, decreased sleep, impulsivity) and depression.

Schizophrenia positive symptoms: Hallucinations (false perceptions — hearing voices) + delusions (fixed false beliefs).
Negative symptoms: Flat affect, poverty of speech, social withdrawal, avolition (lack of motivation).

Borderline PD: Unstable relationships/identity/moods, fear of abandonment, impulsivity, self-harm.
Antisocial PD: Disregard for others' rights, deceitfulness, lack of remorse.

ASD: Deficits in social communication; restricted, repetitive behaviors; varies in severity.
ADHD: Inattention and/or hyperactivity-impulsivity impairing functioning; onset in childhood.

Therapy

Biological therapy vs. psychotherapy; major approaches▶

Biological therapy: Treats disorders with physical/medical means — medications (antidepressants, antipsychotics, anxiolytics), ECT (electroconvulsive therapy for severe depression), TMS (transcranial magnetic stimulation).

TypeFocus
PsychodynamicUnconscious conflicts; free association, dream analysis
HumanisticSelf-acceptance, growth; unconditional positive regard
Behavior therapyChange maladaptive behaviors via conditioning
CBTChange negative thoughts AND behaviors — most evidence-based
Psychodynamic techniques: free association & dream analysis▶

Free association: The client says whatever comes to mind without censoring — the therapist listens for recurring themes, slips, and patterns that reveal unconscious conflicts.

Dream analysis: Freud believed dreams contain both manifest content (what literally happens in the dream) and latent content (the hidden unconscious meaning beneath the surface).

Goal of psychodynamic therapy: Bring unconscious material into conscious awareness → achieve insight → resolve inner conflict and reduce symptoms.

Behavior therapies; token economy; CBT; group therapy▶
  • Client-centered therapy (Rogers): Unconditional positive regard + empathy + genuineness → self-actualization
  • Systematic desensitization: Gradually expose client to feared stimulus while maintaining relaxation (treats phobias)
  • Exposure & response prevention: Confront feared trigger, resist compulsion (treats OCD)
  • Token economy: Desired behaviors earn tokens redeemable for privileges/rewards — used in schools, psychiatric wards, and rehabilitation settings
  • CBT: Identifies and challenges cognitive distortions; changes both thoughts and behaviors. Most empirically supported therapy overall.
  • Group therapy: Multiple clients treated together — social support, shared experience, reduced isolation, cost-effective
